# The Pythia Games

## Preface

Dear Reader,

The Twin Flame connection is a frequency.

They promise reunion. A divine other. A mirror. A reward. That is the holy slogan, anyway. In practice, it grants nothing. It repairs. It binds. It stabilizes something fragile in a world where little Lost Boys would rather break than hold.

I have not seen his face in years. Even that memory did not return until somewhere after the fourth loop. Maybe later. I have been walking so long I do not remember every place I have been until the moment I need to.

Something went wrong in this timeline. I never found the fracture. I do not hunt it the way I used to, but I still circle the wreckage now and then. The result is always the same.

I am early to a union in a timeline that does not want me.

The irony is not lost on me. I am nearly late for everything in this life, yet always early for the one event that matters. Early enough to miss it. Or worse, early enough to watch it fracture.

There are already two here, living out the Nikola Tesla and Thomas Edison loop. The frequency that finds us every lifetime does not care about gender. It is the embodiment of both sides at once. Divine masculine and divine feminine collapsed into a single channel.

This time, the grid glitched.

There were not supposed to be three of us.
There were not supposed to be two of them.

And of course it had to be the pair who always have the least to give to love: men who pour everything they have into innovation and infrastructure, starving the parts of themselves that know how to stay.

When I dropped into this timeline, everything split wrong.

This is the first life where I was conscious for The Remembering.

It was not poetic. It was labor without the mercy of narcotics. No relief, just pressure and passage. Memory tearing its way back through flesh.

The first life that came back to me belonged to Aspasia and Socrates. Her indignation was so precise she could drop a gadfly from across the room without interrupting her argument. Being in love felt like cool salt water, decadent finger foods, and rebel war cries shouted into the night.

I have lost count of the loops.
I have lost count of the rejections.

Jesus and Mary Magdalene. Aspasia and Socrates. Hades and Persephone. Tesla and Edison. Me and Zeke.

I carry the scars from the first time. You cannot see them. The divine thread does not just love; it restores. I am more beautiful than ever.

The scars are still there, though. They move under my skin like snakes in water. Get too close and they bite.

So this is the story I will tell you. Not because I want to. I will have to live it again to tell it. My body will yearn, jerk, pull, resist, reach. It always remembers before I do.

I will tell it because some things burn until they become inevitable. Until they build and tighten and recalibrate. Until you either breathe fire or rot from the heat.

One pass to raze.
One pass to seed.

The memories will not be pleasant.
The body never forgets.

You will feel things before you understand them—
just like I did.

Your pulse will quicken in places that do not make sense.
Your skin will recognize scenes your mind has never lived.
Your stomach will drop at doors you do not remember walking through.

That is how remembering works.

You might not like me.
It does not matter.

Your body will answer before your mind decides.

So tell me—
what would you write
if you finally remembered?

Because remembering never starts at the beginning.

Everything is later. Some sweeter. Some bitter enough to tune the spiral tighter. We begin in the middle of the old world, in the loop where I found my Lost Boys. Or where they found me. The story changes depending on who tells it.

A frequency made us recognize each other.
Choice kept us together.

I am not soft enough to be a Wendy. Not a woman with lullabies and thimbles. I have scales. Two sets of teeth. There is only one name for that.

Dragon.

Call me whatever you want. Just understand this:
the Lost Boys do not follow Peter anymore.

—Ninneva

## Chapter One
### Ninneva

The metal is warm under my bruised thighs, the kind of heat only a machine in motion gives off. A memetic rush. A sensual, somatic reminder that velocity still answers me, though now it answers with impatience, like it knows I do not have time to stop.

I really do not.

Nowhere, West Texas, is not a place you linger, even before the drop. Now it is worse: east of somewhere, west of nowhere.

And yet.

The dead wolf in the ditch makes the decision for me.

She is female. Bloody. Thrown there like refuse. Not just dead. Used. Whatever she was meant to hold, whatever adjustment she stood between and the rest of the grid, it was hard won. Judging by the arc of torn fur crowning the ground around her, I would say she held them for seventeen minutes.

That is a long time alone.

Light fur lies scattered at her feet, offerings thrown down by a lover who came too late.

You do not take a wolf's throat alone.
You do it in packs.

These days there are more coyotes than players. That is the sad math of this timeline. Nowhere is safe anymore. Not the ground. Not the sky. When the divine channel dropped out, when the frequency fell too low to carry meaning, hope did not fade.

It vanished overnight.

I know how this sounds. Like I have wandered off into theory.

I have not.

The dead wolf and the frequency collapse are the same event. I felt her days ago. The pressure in my ears. The double-heart pounding its warning. I did not need to find her. This is not a marker that requires my eyes to advance the spiral.

But when a lone wolf falls, you show respect to the honest heart, even if it was not pure.

You honor the thread ripped from an already thinning network. You acknowledge the fight.

And she fought.

Teeth met flesh more than once. Her body is mapped with proof that she did not go quietly.

I do not decide to bury the wolf.

My hands are already clearing the ground before the thought finishes forming. Loose stones moved aside. A branch lifted and set back where it was found.

Not efficiency.
Courtesy.

I pause once, palm flat against the dirt, feeling for something that is not temperature. The contact steadies me. I hold the breath a fraction longer than needed, exhale, and begin.

The ground gives more easily than it should.

I remove my watch and set it face-down on a rock without looking at it. Time does not belong here.

The digging is quiet. No frantic tearing. No haste. My body knows the depth. Knows when enough is enough. Knows when the space matches the weight it is meant to hold.

I do not drag her.
I lift her.

Carefully.

The blood has already cooled, tacky against fur. The throat is worse up close. I do not flinch. Flinching would be disrespect.

I lower the wolf into the earth and adjust her once, just once, so the spine lies straight. No twisting. No contortion. Whatever happened at the end does not get to rearrange her now.

My hands hesitate over the body for a beat, then withdraw.

Soil returns in layers, the way it came out. I press it down with both palms, evenly, sealing rather than hiding. Closing something properly instead of abandoning it half-done.

When I finish, the ground looks almost untouched.

Almost.

Something in me recognizes the posture. The angle of my wrists. The absence of words.

Two wolves, my grandmother says, not as a lesson but as a statement of fact. They both live in you. You do not starve one. You learn which one eats first.

There was more. There was always more. Sometimes a bear. Sometimes a burial. Sometimes a warning about what happens when you pretend you are only feeding one.

That part never stuck cleanly.

What stayed was this: feeding is an act. Not a feeling. Not a belief. An allocation.

The wolf in the ground is not one of the two.

I know that without thinking it through. This is not hunger. This is not rage. This is the cost of letting the fight exist at all. The loss that makes the story possible and never gets counted.

I offer nothing else. No marker. No promise.

The care was the answer.

When I turn away, the ground holds.

Then I see it.

The line.

So thin you would miss it if you were not looking for boundaries. A perfect hold carved into dirt and instinct.

She did not die running.
She died standing.

I follow the near-invisible path down the embankment by the river. What I find in the small, warm den below changes everything.

Strays.

There is only one pattern that starts this way.

I lower myself to their level, letting my energy settle before I move an inch closer. Once they tune, we are gone. No debate. No delay.

I have a new world to write.

This spiral will be brutal.
It has to be.

I am strong enough now that Zeke does not waste my energy unless the damage is catastrophic, one of those failures that requires the entire circuit to reroute. Otherwise he avoids me. Hearing my voice scrambles his neural map.

I should be used to this cycle by now.

I am.
Mostly.

Whatever the Council broke by meddling with the Fates did not just fracture me. It hit everyone.

Back in my favorite machine, two male pups riding shotgun, my mind spins through every life where this pattern appeared. Every failure. Every broken heart. The rare successes where I walked the spiral to the end and he did too.

I linger on those.

I like the charge they leave behind. Love is efficient fuel, even when it burns.

I would never admit that to his face in this timeline.

Still, the countdown starts.

He will step into frame soon. Whether the energy wears his face this time is another question entirely. The answer will tell me exactly how this spiral ends.

Crispy.
Charred.
Or phoenix, clawing back through ash.

Ah.

I am warm already.

I hit the gas as the puppies start roaming loose in the car, restless and alive, a rude little contrast to the death still buzzing in my chest. My heart squeezes tight, like a woman curling her toes.

That is when the resonance slips in.

Not grief-triggered.
Aftermath-triggered.

The quiet after the act.

The road smooths. The engine note stretches. A memory surfaces: the first time she struck a note. No audience. No meaning yet. Just vibration continuing without permission.

### Ezekiel (Zeke)

Zeke is not in the house with her.

He is in the house because of her.

He built it before she arrived. Before he knew how to recognize the interval that precedes her. The structure finished itself the night the grid learned her name again, though he would not have phrased it that way then. He only knew the measurements stopped changing. The tolerances locked.

The room stopped asking questions.

Tonight, the pressure equalizes.

Not grief. Not alarm. No spike. Just a held imbalance choosing direction. Like a seal breaking clean instead of tearing.

He exhales without realizing he had been holding anything.

He does not think wolf.

He thinks boundary.

A line held longer than designed. A line released precisely enough to be reused.

That registers first.

The space behind the bookcase responds before he does. Veins in the walls dim, tighten, reroute. Not retreating. Recognizing. He has learned the difference. Death is noise. Death throws heat and static and demands witnesses.

This is geometry.

He steps into the room and the field resolves around him, the way it only does when his coherence matches the build. Anyone else would see clever carpentry and a hidden latch. For him, the room becomes a live interface.

Not imagined.
Not imposed.

It meets him halfway or not at all.

He does not reach for her.
He does not narrate her.

He marks time.

A glance at the far wall. A calibration check he cannot name but never skips. The third hinge has gone cold.

That is new.

Or rather, old enough to be back again.

He files it without reaction.

The dangerous resets are not loud.

What lands is not she is in danger.

It is worse.

The game has acknowledged her.

That means movement. That means the board is no longer theoretical.

He waits, because waiting is the only action that does not distort the signal.

Memory creeps in without invitation. Not images. Corrections.

A prior restart where he was slower. One where he argued for causality while she was already operating in consequence. Another where he tried to protect the structure instead of listening to what it was accommodating. He almost lost her that way.

Not to death.
To misalignment.

Harder to recover from.

He learned the rule after.

When she names it first, you do not ask what died.
You ask what is coming back.

The room finishes rerouting. The veins settle into a configuration he has not seen since the last quiet collapse. He adjusts the field a fraction. Clears a seat. Leaves space where space will be needed.

He does not rush to her.

He prepares.

Whatever she just named, she did it alone. He can feel the asymmetry without resentment. She is ahead again.

Not admiration.
Not fear.

Fact.

That is why this works.

His role is not to lead or follow. It is to hold continuity while she collapses loops. He stays so the structure does not tear itself apart when she moves.

He has not seen the body. He has not heard the story. He only feels the grid slip a fraction off true, enough to know something has been properly closed.

The room recognizes it before his mind does.

He lets it.

She will arrive when she is ready. She always does. And when she does, they will not be meeting as strangers.

He waits in the room because he knows she will need it.

He never knows why.

The structure holds.

Then something else resolves.

Not arrival.
Not memory.

Alignment.

The pressure in the room does not change, but its orientation does. The architecture remains exactly where it is, and yet the space between objects loosens, like a thought finishing itself without words.

Zeke does not move.
He does not have to.

This is not the room he built.

It is the one that answers when he stays still long enough.

The oak table is scarred in places no one remembers causing. Old heat marks. Knife slips. Rings set down too hard. It is a good surface. Honest.

They sit across from each other, close enough that the space between them already feels negotiated. Two glasses. The liquid inside is warm bronze, catching light without trying to hold it.

“Cheers,” he says.

She taps her glass against his. Not ceremonial. Accurate.

“Cheers to last first kisses.”

She snorts, because of course she does.

He feels the relief immediately. Humor as pressure release. He takes the opening, sets his glass down, keeps his hand on the table where she can see it.

“Come sit on my lap.”

No flourish. No test. An invitation stated like a fact.

She takes his hand and lets him pull her in. The movement is quick, decisive. Her weight settles against him and something in his chest locks into place. His hands find her hips and stay there, gripping just hard enough that he has to pay attention to it.

“If you do not want me to kiss you,” he says, steady, “now is the time to say so.”

She says something sharp and funny. A deflection, but not a retreat. He recognizes it. Lets it pass.

She leans back a fraction, still smiling, watching candlelight catch in his eyes.

He does not wait.

The kiss is immediate. No ramp. No warning. Like a line finally closing. Her mouth opens under his and her tongue meets his with intent, not curiosity.

She is not afraid of the intensity.

That matters to him more than the kiss.

She pulls back just enough to speak, lips still brushing his.

“I can feel you smiling.”

He laughs, low, because she is right. “Sorry to dive-bomb you.”

“You are not sorry.”

“No,” he admits. “I couldn't bear to see another one of your smiles without tasting it.”

She tilts her head, considering him, eyes bright.

“What did it taste like?”

He does not answer immediately. He presses his forehead to hers instead, breath syncing without effort.

“Recognition,” he says finally. “And restraint.”

Her smile shifts. Slower. More dangerous.

“Good,” she says. “Because I am not done smiling.”

He tightens his hold on her hips, grounding himself.

Not because he is losing control.
Because he is choosing to keep it.

This is not the beginning.

It is the point where beginning becomes inevitable.


## Chapter Two
### Ninneva

The beach is empty in the way only working beaches ever are. Too much wind. Too much undertow. Sand that does not flatter anyone who wants to lie still and be admired.

Perfect.

She leaves her clothes in a loose pile above the tide line. Not hidden. Not displayed. Just where they fall.

The sun takes her immediately, salt-sticky and bright, like it has been waiting. She walks down until the water hits mid-thigh, then turns and lowers herself into the sand, letting it mold around her hips and spine.

The field opens.

Not outward. Inward first. A tightening, then release, like pressure finding its own channels. She does not summon it. She never does.

The field responds to stillness the way predators respond to silence.

Surfers drift in from the south, one by one, boards cutting clean lines across the water. They do not look at her at first. That is how she knows it is working.

When the field is loud, it attracts attention.
When it is coherent, it attracts motion.

They claim the stretch of water in front of her without speaking. A quiet agreement. A pocket forms. Waves organize themselves differently there, standing a fraction longer before breaking. Enough to ride. Enough to matter.

She smiles once and lets her head fall back.

Heat pools low in her body, dense and patient. Not hunger. Not fantasy. Load. The kind of charge that builds in systems designed to conduct more than one current at a time. She breathes through it, slow, letting it split where it needs to split.

The Lost Boys feel it before they understand it.

They always do.

A flicker of attention. A sharpening. Each one tuning to a slightly different band. One leans too far forward on his board, corrects. Another rides longer than he should, eats sand, comes up laughing.

The field opens a layer deeper, threads weaving without tangling.

No grabbing.
No drain.

A clean boundary snaps into place and holds.

She relaxes then.
Finally.

This is why the beach works. Enough motion outside her to still what lives inside. Enough rhythm to carry excess away. The field hums, calibrated, letting the younger ones brush the edge without cutting themselves open.

She does not touch them.

She does not need to.

Her system registers everything anyway. Excitement. Attraction. The reflexive pull toward something that does not ask to be explained. She lets it pass through, warm and bright, like fire through a flue built for it.

Deep under the sand, something answers.

Not a voice. Not a thought. A pressure, old and mineral, like veins running through stone. She feels it where heat meets structure, where power divides without diminishing. One current feeding another. Two heads drawing from the same source, neither canceling the other out.

She exhales, long.

The waterline creeps closer. A wave breaks farther in than expected, wetting her calves. She digs her heels into the sand and lets the tide take the edge off the heat, just enough to keep it from tipping.

She could burn the whole shoreline if she wanted.

She never wants to.

A surfer glances back, finally, eyes catching, curious. She meets his gaze, unbothered, and lets the field acknowledge him without invitation. He nods once, like he has been given permission he did not know he was asking for, and turns back to the water.

Good.

She stays until the sun shifts and the field begins to close on its own, threads loosening, charge dispersing into the wider grid. The Lost Boys drift away as they came, none the worse for it. Some better.

When she stands, sand clings to her thighs and hips, grounding the last of the heat. She brushes it off with slow hands, unhurried, and walks back up the beach.

Then the pull tugs at her chest.

Sharp.
Specific.

Zeke.

She stills, one hand curling briefly into the sand, then releases it. She does not follow the pull yet. She lets it register. Lets it burn clean instead of spreading.

West is coming.

She gathers her clothes and leaves the beach as quietly as she arrived, the field sealing behind her like water over a submerged fire.

Zeke

The office is already awake when Zeke gets there, which means he is late by his own standards. Screens glow. Papers shift. The building hums the way it always does when everything is technically functioning.

None of it is the infrastructure that matters anymore.

Eli is barefoot, perched sideways in a chair like he never learned how furniture works, coffee balanced dangerously on one knee. Peter stands behind him, jacket still on, reading over his shoulder and shaking his head.

“You look like shit,” Eli says, without looking up. “Good morning.”

Zeke drops his keys on the table harder than necessary. “I slept.”

“Yeah,” Peter says. “So do raccoons.”

Zeke ignores that and sits. He presses his palm flat against his chest without thinking. The pressure there has not eased since last night. If anything, it is sharper.

Not pain.
Compression.

Like something vital got wrapped too tight.

Eli finally looks at him. His grin softens a notch. “Okay. You are not normal-edgy. You are new edgy.”

Peter nods. “You have been snapping at vendors. You told the city liaison to fuck himself yesterday.”

“He did fuck himself,” Zeke says.

“Sure,” Peter says. “But usually you enjoy the long game.”

Zeke leans back and stares at the ceiling. The urge hits him then, hard enough to make his jaw clench. The instinct to go to her. To put his hands on her shoulders, her hips, her spine. Sometimes it grabs him by the throat. Sometimes lower.

Today it is his heart in a vice.

That is new.

“There is no point in dating,” he says finally.

Eli snorts. “That is not what we asked.”

“I only get hard for one woman,” Zeke continues, flat. “And she is not available in any way that would make the apps useful.”

Peter and Eli exchange a look.

Zeke notices. Pushes. “What.”

Eli sighs. “Man. You have been saying her name in your sleep again.”

Zeke stills. “You do not know what you are hearing.”

“We know enough,” Peter says carefully. “And you are not wrong about the pull. Whatever you did last night—”

“I did not do anything.”

“Whatever happened,” Peter amends, “it tightened something. You are vibrating.”

Zeke lets that sit. Then, quieter: “She named something.”

Eli's expression shifts. Less joking now. “Is that bad?”

“It means movement,” Zeke says. “And movement collapses structures if no one stays to hold continuity.”

“So go to her,” Eli says. Immediate. Simple. “Stop white-knuckling this.”

“I cannot leave,” Zeke says. “If I go, the field collapses here. One of us always has to stay.”

That gets another look between them. Longer this time.

Zeke straightens. “Don't.”

Peter raises his hands slightly. “Hear us out.”

Eli leans forward, elbows on knees. “We have been thinking about this for a while. You are not as subtle as you think.”

Zeke's voice goes cold. “She is not a problem to be solved.”

“We know,” Eli says quickly. “Jesus. That's not—”

Peter finishes it. “If both of us connected to her too. Not like you. Not replacing anything. Just anchoring. Then you could leave. We could hold the field here until you come back together.”

The room goes quiet.

Zeke does not respond right away. His protectiveness flares sharp and immediate, followed by something else.

Reluctant recognition.

“You do not understand what you are offering,” he says.

Eli meets his gaze, steady. “We understand exactly enough.”

Peter nods. “The world fears men who will not bend. It fears her more. That is the only reason this works.”

Zeke exhales slowly. The pressure in his chest shifts. It does not ease. It changes shape.

He looks at them.

Really looks.

“Nothing touches her without consent,” he says.

“Of course,” Eli says.

“And if this destabilizes you—”

“We tell you,” Peter says. “Before it becomes damage.”

Zeke stands. Walks to the window. Watches the city pretend it is not sitting on fault lines.

He does not say yes.
He does not say no.

But the idea lodges.

And that is how he knows it is real.

Ninneva

The night does not announce itself.

No sirens. No weather. No omen dressed up as coincidence.

Just a street doing what streets do. Bodies moving. Laughter leaking out of doorways. Music bleeding thin through brick. The grid holds steady the way it always has, stretched tight enough that no one notices the strain anymore.

Ninneva stands in it without bracing.

She feels the tension long before it finds language. A load shifting somewhere below threshold. The kind of pressure that usually resolves quietly or breaks something small to stay intact.

This time, it does not resolve.

She does not reach for the field. She does not pull. She does not prepare. Preparation would imply choice, and this is not that.

Something is aligning too fast.

Around her, people slow without knowing why. A laugh cuts off mid-breath. A glass hesitates on its way to a mouth. For a fraction of a second, the world behaves like it is waiting for permission to continue.

Ninneva stays upright.

She does not think now.
She does not think this.

She only notices that the grid has gone very, very quiet.

She does not fall.

## Chapter Three
### Ninneva

The flicker lasts one second.

Not enough to study. Not enough to prove.

Long enough to scar.

For one breath, the underlayer surfaces.

Shadows lift off the ground like they remember having wings. Skin misaligns with what it has been containing. Forms bleed through forms, higher geometries pressing against human outlines without permission or apology.

Hybrids everywhere are caught mid-translation.

Most people never see it cleanly.

They feel it instead.

A drop. A vertigo. Knees buckling. Vision stuttering. Phones slipping from hands. The body reacting faster than the mind can invent a reason.

Some black out.
Some scream.
Some collapse where they stand, systems overwhelmed by a signal never meant for conscious reception.

The grid corrects itself immediately.

Of course it does.

But correction does not erase what happened. It seals it behind denial, footage glitches, shared confusion. The kind of event history will argue about forever without touching the truth.

Power groups register the spike all at once.

They do not agree on what it means, but the conclusions converge fast:

The grid is alive.
Someone tried to collapse it.
The hybrids can no longer pass unnoticed.
And one person stayed conscious when the grid surged hard enough to kill weaker systems.

Ninneva is still standing.

Not elevated.
Not crowned.
Not spared.

Just present.

Watching the afterimage fade while the world around her tries to convince itself nothing happened.

She exhales.

The rest of us now have to stand.
